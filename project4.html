CFD of Airfoil E423 (ANSYS Fluent)

Project — Aerodynamic Performance, Verification & Flow Physics

University of British Columbia

Summary

This project analyzes the Eppler E423 airfoil in ANSYS Fluent to quantify lift, drag, and key flow features versus angle of attack (AoA) and Mach number. The E423 is a cambered, low-Reynolds-number airfoil used in small UAVs and RC sailplanes for its high lift and gentle pre-stall behavior.

I ran steady density-based simulations with the k–ω SST turbulence model for subsonic and transonic conditions. Subsonic cases (e.g., M = 0.3 at AoA 2°–8°) exhibit a nearly linear increase in lift with AoA, a strong suction region near the leading edge, and a viscous wake that thickens with angle. A transonic case (M = 0.9, AoA ≈ 4°) shows shock-induced pressure jumps and separation onset. A higher-AoA subsonic case (M = 0.3, AoA = 15°) highlights periodic wake shedding, captured by velocity vectors and vorticity.

What?

Evaluate lift and drag trends of E423 across AoA for subsonic and a transonic point; visualize pressure, velocity, vectors and vorticity to explain the physics behind the Cl and Cd behavior.

How?

Density-based steady solver (ANSYS Fluent) with the k–ω SST turbulence model. Mesh and boundary conditions were tuned for stable solutions across AoA = 2°–15° (M = 0.3) and a transonic snapshot (M = 0.9, AoA ≈ 4°). Field plots (pressure/velocity contours, vectors, vorticity) were used to connect flow features to integrated coefficients.

Results

Subsonic Cl increases almost linearly pre-stall; Cd rises with AoA as the wake thickens. The transonic case shows a shock on the suction side and initial separation, while the AoA = 15° case exhibits periodic wake structures. The integrated Cl and Cd trends match the field-plot physics, providing a coherent aerodynamic story.

Pressure contour — AoA = 2°, M = 0.3.

At low AoA, suction forms near the leading edge with mild adverse gradient toward the trailing edge. The blue indicates areas of low pressure, and the red indicates high pressure. Attached flow is maintained, giving low drag. This pressure distribution sets up the nearly linear portion of the Cl–AoA curve.

Velocity contour — AoA = 2°, M = 0.3.

The velocity field shows acceleration over the upper surface and a thin wake. The blue indicates low velocity and red indicates high velocity. With increasing AoA, this wake thickens and contributes to a gradual rise in Cd.

Pressure contour — AoA = 4°, M = 0.3.

At AoA = 4° (subsonic), the suction peak intensifies and the adverse pressure gradient region grows. The wake becomes thicker, increasing Cd; Cl continues to rise in a near-linear fashion pre-stall.

Velocity contour — transonic snapshot (M = 0.9, AoA ≈ 4°).

The transonic case shows a shock on the suction side with sharp pressure/velocity jumps and initial separation. This is consistent with increased losses and a steeper rise in drag.

Velocity vectors — AoA = 15°, M = 0.3.

At high AoA, vectors highlight separated flow and roll-up of structures in the near wake. This flow state aligns with higher drag and the onset of unsteady features.

Vorticity magnitude — AoA = 15°, M = 0.3.

Coherent vortical structures convect downstream, indicating periodic shedding. This explains the unsteady wake behavior and complements the velocity-vector view.

Cl vs AoA — near-linear growth pre-stall with the expected slope for a cambered low-Re airfoil.
Cd vs AoA — drag increases with AoA due to thicker wake and greater viscous losses.
